# Differential expression and functional analysis of yeast transcriptomic data


## **1 | Introduction**
The advent of RNA sequencing (RNA-Seq) has resulted in a surge of high-resolution gene expression data (Marioni et al., 2008), with this data covering a much greater part of the transcriptome compared to data generated by other methods like Sanger sequencing or microarray methods (Kukurba & Montgomery, 2015). Consequently, the technique of differential gene expression (DGE) analysis, which determines if and what genes are differentially expressed between one group and another, has arisen (Rosati et al., 2024). RNA-Seq, in conjunction with DGE analysis and other methods, can thus identify if genes are involved in a biological process or experimental condition, allowing for important insights to be discovered. Notably, RNA-Seq has led to the discovery of previously unknown gene structures, of diverse splice variants, and of the biased expression of specific alleles (Kukurba & Montgomery, 2015), and has elucidated details on the up- or downregulation of genes. 

The negative binomial distribution is typically used to model RNA-Seq data as it accounts for the biological variation between replicates. As such, parametric methods such as edgeR and DESeq2 are commonly used to analyze RNA-Seq data in order to ensure statistical accuracy (Rosati et al., 2024). Contrasting the two methods, DESeq2 is more conservative than edgeR and generally has better control for the false discovery rate (Soneson & Delorenzi, 2013). Moreover, edgeR showed poorer control of the false positive rate when tested with yeast transcriptome data (Nookaew et al., 2012). Additionally, DESeq2 is more typically employed for data obtained from controlled experimental conditions, while edgeR shows better performance with more complex and highly variable data (Rosati et al., 2024).

Before DGE analysis however, counts of the transcripts must be quantified. This can be done through alignment-based methods like STAR and Bowtie2, which quantify counts from an aligned transcriptome or genome. Alternatively, pseudoaligners like Salmon and kallisto can perform quantification without alignment (Svrivastava et al., 2020). Recently, pseudoaligners appear to outperform traditional alignment-based methods in factors such as accuracy and speed (Yi et al., 2018). There are also distinctions to be made between pseudoaligners, as methods like selective alignment can be performed via Salmon, but not kallisto (Svrivastava et al., 2020). In selective alignment, decoy sequences are used, which not only reduce false positives but also outperform quasi-mapping and alignment-based methods (Svrivastava et al., 2020). Salmon, when compared to kallisto and other pseudoaligners, includes models that account for sequence, GC, and positional biases (Patro et al., 2014).

Post-quantification and DGE analysis, functional annotation and the corresponding enrichment analysis is performed to map genes to biological processes. Enrichment analysis involves the identification of pathways and processes that are enriched, allowing for association of differentially expressed genes to biological functions (Svrivastava et al., 2020). To perform functional annotation, one or multiple databases are used to associate genes. Since over-representation analysis will be performed via clusterProfiler, the biological processes of genes will be annotated via Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG. The R package clusterProfiler already integrates these databases (Yu et al., 2012), but using GO and KEGG also provide other benefits. GO can provide annotations for biological processes, while KEGG can map metabolic pathways (Svrivasta et al., 2020). Taken together, the biological patterns of differentially expressed genes and their influence on the processes within the organism can be discovered.

In this project, _Saccharomyces cerevisiae_ flor strain transcripts obtained from the study conducted by Mardanov et al. (2020) will be quantified and analyzed to determine differentially expressed genes and enrichment of genes in biological processes and metabolic pathways. The flor yeast transcripts reflect biological replicates from three distinct stages of velum development during the aging of wine. Biofilm formation allows yeast to access oxygen for efficient oxidative metabolism (Alexandre, 2013), in turn allowing the yeast to survive in harsh conditions evoked by alcoholic fermentation such as low O2 concentration and high ethanol concentration (Alexandre, 2013). Not only does the biofilm provide protection for the yeast and facilitate proliferation, it also has the added benefit of protecting the wine from oxidation (Moreno-Garcia et al., 2017). Notably, flor yeasts undergo changes in gene expression during stages of biofilm formation in response to changing environmental conditions, metabolic shifts, and responses to stressors (Moreno-Garcia et al., 2017; Mardanov et al., 2020). Thus, studying the differential expression of genes during velum formation will provide insights into the biological processes and metabolic pathways activated or repressed as yeast responds to its environment. Given the advantages provided by Salmon, DESeq2, and clusterProfiler for quantification, DGE analysis, and functional annotation respectively, these will be the main methods used in the analytical pipeline.


## **2 | Methods**

### **2.1 | Data Description**
The transcripts for the yeast dataset were obtained from the [BioProject page (Accession: PRJNA592304)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/bioproject/PRJNA592304), which was generated from the study conducted by Mardanov et al. (2020). In total, nine SRA files were downloaded and converted to FASTQ format using SRA Toolkit (v3.2.1). The reference genome, transcriptome, and GTF annotation file for _Saccharomyces cerevisiae (strain S288C)_ was obtained from the NCBI genome database. These files were renamed to ref.fna, rna.fna, and genomic.gtf, respectively.

```
prefetch SRR105516{57..65}
fasterq-dump SRR105516{57..65}

datasets download genome accession GCF_000146045.2 --include genome,rna,gtf --filename yeast_ref.zip
```

### **2.2 | Quality Control**
FastQC (v0.12.1) was used to inspect the quality of the reads pre-quantification. The resulting fastqc.html files were combined into a single report using MultiQC (v1.33). According to the summary figures generated by MultiQC, the reads had high per base quality scores, with most exceeding a Phred score of 35 (Fig. 1). Although the summary heatmap reported unusual results for per base sequence content and sequence duplication levels (Fig. 2), these are expected for RNASeq reads, as they reflect biases or artifacts in library preparation (Shi et al., 2021). Moreover, trimming reads appears to have no discernible effect on the accuracy of quantification (Liao & Shi, 2020). As such, no trimming or pre-filtering was performed pre-quantification.
```
fastqc *.fastq -o ./fastqc_results
cd fastqc_results/
multiqc ./
```

### **2.3 | Quantification**
Quantification of the transcripts was performed using Salmon (v1.10.3). A full selective alignment paradigm, as described by Srivastava et al. (2020), was used during quantification. This involved creating a decoy file with the reference genome as the decoy sequences. An index was created using the concatenated transcriptome and genome, along with the decoy sequences.
```
grep "^>" ref/ref.fna | cut -d " " -f 1 > decoys.txt
sed -i 's/>//g' decoys.txt
cat ref/rna.fna ref/ref.fna > genome_transcripts.fa
salmon index -t genome_transcripts.fa -d decoys.txt -p 6 -i transcripts_index
```
Salmon was then used to quantify counts for each read. The script used to iterate over the reads is described in [quantify_reads.sh](scripts/quantify_reads.sh). The flags --gcBias and --seqBias were employed to mitigate the effects of potential GC bias and sequence bias in the reads.
```
salmon quant -i transcripts_index -l A -r ../reads/${read}.fastq -p 6 --validateMappings --gcBias --seqBias -o quants/${read}
```


### **2.4 | Differential Gene Expression Analysis and Functional Annotation**
Differential expression analysis and functional enrichment analysis were conducted on the quantified counts generated by Salmon. The full pipeline and code used for these analyses and the resulting figures is described in [yeast_transcriptome_analysis.R](scripts/yeast_transcriptome_analysis.R). The DESeq2 package (v1.50.2) was used to generate differential gene expression results. The design formula included stage (i.e. early, thin, mature) as the factor. Log fold change shrinkage was then performed on the resulting DESeqDataSet objects. Pairwise comparisons were performed (e.g. thin vs early, mature vs early, mature vs thin).
```
dds_TXI <- DESeqDataSetFromTximport(txi,
  colData = df_yeast_metadata,
  design = ~stage
)
dds_e <- DESeq(dds_TXI)

evt_LFC <- lfcShrink(dds_e,
  coef = "stage_thin_vs_early",
  type = "apeglm"
)
```
Functional annotation and over-representation analysis was conducted using the clusterProfiler package (v 4.18.4). Both GO and KEGG enrichment were performed to determine any overrepresented biological processes or metabolic pathways in each stage.

### **2.5 | Visualization of Results**
Results from the DGE and ORA were visualized using several packages: ggplot2 (v4.0.2), ggpubr (v0.6.3), EnhancedVolcano (v1.28.2), pheatmap (v1.0.13), enrichplot (v1.31.4), and cowplot (v1.2.0).

### **3.1 | Overall Data Structure**
A PCA plot and a heatmap were created to visualize the overall structure of the transcriptomic data post-quantification and pre-analysis. As seen in the PCA plot, the replicates cluster well together, and the experimental groups (i.e. stages) are separate from each other (Fig. 3). PC1 explains 72% of the variance in the data, with the groups showing no overlap along this axis. PC2 explains 24% of the variation, with the thin stage separated from the early and mature stages along the y-axis. The heatmap shows the same pattern seen in the PCA plot, with the samples clustering together by stage (Fig. 4). Sequences from each stage are distinct from each other, as represented by the darker orange and red colors. Sequences in the early and mature samples are similar among themselves, while sequences in the thin samples show slightly more variability.

### **3.2 | Differential Gene Expression**
Two MA plots for each pairwise comparison were created, with top genes being labeled according to either the adjusted P-value or the log fold change. Colored points were filtered as statistically significant (p < 0.05), and further divided into upregulated (red) and blue (downregulated) with a cutoff log fold change value of 2. In the thin vs early comparison, there were 485 upregulated genes and 448 downregulated genes, for a total of 933 significant genes. In the mature vs thin comparison, there were 1086 significant genes (627 upregulated and 459 downregulated), while in the mature vs early comparison, there were 1715 significant genes (951 upregulated and 764 downregulated). Although each comparison had the same number of samples, the early vs mature comparison appears to have a greater proportion of statistically significant genes, as seen in Figure 6. Certain genes appear to be consistently downregulated in specific stages, such as YHR094C, YGR087C, and YGR296W in the thin and mature stages (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). Conversely, YCR105W is upregulated in the thin stage, while YEL070W is upregulated in the mature stage. The volcano plot for the mature vs thin comparison has a smaller scale for the y-axis (going up to 80), compared to the other comparisons (going up to 150). The heatmap shows the overall pattern of gene expression levels between the different stages (Fig. 7). Generally, genes upregulated in one stage are downregulated in the others, as demonstrated by the block of genes differentially expressed in the early stage compared to the mature stage.

### **3.3 | Functional Annotation**
Dot plots were used to visualize the upregulation or downregulation of genes in the three stages, with genes functionally annotated to a biological process or a metabolic pathway. In the early stage, there is a consistent upregulation of genes involved in glycolytic processes, transmembrane transport, or nucleotide metabolism and catabolism (Fig. 8), with an enrichment of genes in amino acid and sugar metabolic/catabolic pathways as well as molecule biosynthesis (Fig. 9). The thin stage displays upregulation in processes and pathways related to oxidative metabolism (e.g. metabolism of fatty acids, carbon, and pyruvate) and lipid metabolic/biosynthetic processes (Fig. 8, 9). In the mature stage, upregulated genes are enriched in energy-generating and mitochondrion-related processes (Fig.8) and pathways in molecular metabolism, oxidative phosphorylation, and the ribosome (Fig. 9).

## **4 | Discussion**
The transcriptomic data obtained from the study appear to be of good quality, as replicates cluster according to stage (Fig. 3, 4). This clustering suggests that each experimental group has a distinct transcriptomic profile, with different collections of genes expressed during each stage of velum formation. PC1, now identified as the stage, explains 72% of the variation in the dataset (Fig. 3). Replicates in the early stage and mature stage are greatly separated along this component, reflecting the much greater variability in gene expression levels between the early and mature stages. Notably, the PCA plot shows a V-shaped trend, with the thin stage separated from the early and mature stages. PC2 may then reflect a transcriptomic shift as yeast shifts away from glycolysis and moves towards oxidative metabolism (Gutierrez, Roldan, & Pérez, 2010), thus expressing a set of gluconeogenic or stress-related genes distinct from those in the early and mature stages. This is supported by the heatmap (Fig. 4), which shows that replicates in the thin stage show greater variability within themselves. In comparison, the early and mature replicates demonstrate greater in-group similarity. It is key to note that overall, each stage's replicates are distinct and well-separated from replicates of other stages.

Differential gene expression and functional enrichment analyses of the yeast transcripts then provide interesting biological insights as to the expression of genes during each stage of velum formation. The stages of velum formation are marked by changes in environmental conditions, which consequently place pressure on the yeast to respond. As such, changes in the expression of genes reflect yeast's adaptations. Between all pairwise comparisons, the mature vs early comparison has the greatest number of statistically significant genes that also exceed the log fold change cutoff (Fig. 5). This reflects the fact that these two stages are maximally separated by time and physiological changes, and would thus have a distinct set of differentially expressed genes that map to different biological processes. The mature vs early comparison also has the most significant genes that exhibit large log fold changes (Fig. 6), demonstrating that a large number of genes are substantially upregulated or downregulated between the two conditions. It is also key to address that the smaller scale used in the mature vs thin comparison highlights that the transcriptomic profiles of these two stages are more similar (Fig. 6). Overall, the early and mature stages are transcriptionally very different, while the thin stage serves as a transition state, thus being more transcriptionally similar to the early and mature stages.

Focusing on the top genes (Fig. 5-7) reveals the general biological processes and metabolic pathways in the stages of biofilm formation. Upregulated genes in the early stage are enriched in biological processes and pathways related to glycolysis, nucleotide/amino acid metabolism, and transmembrane transport. Thus, the early stage reflects the initial establishment of yeast as it utilizes glycolysis and pyruvate decarboxylation for energy, given the abundance of glucose and other nutrients in its environment (Fig. 8, 9). Notably, the pathway of biosynthesis of secondary metabolites is significantly enriched in early upregulated genes, corresponding to yeast's active production of molecules in its early fermentation. Genes such as YGR088, YGR087, YH094C, and YOR273C are upregulated in the early stage (downregulated in thin/mature) (Fig. 5, 6). These genes are induced in high glucose and ethanol concentrations, and are broadly involved in glycolysis and alcoholic fermentation processes. For instance, YH094C/HXT1 encodes a glucose transporter for uptake of glucose when environmental glucose is high (Roy et al., 2015). YGR087/PDC6 encodes a pyruvate decarboxylase, which is required for alcoholic fermentation (Mardanove et al., 2020). These genes' consequent downregulation in the thin and mature stages reflects the shift away from glycolysis and fermentation and towards oxidative metabolism. The upregulation of YGR088 in the early stage reflects yeasts' response to the initial concentration of ethanol in its environment, as this gene encodes a catalase which protects against oxidative stress (Grant, Perrone, & Dawes, 1998). YOR273C/TPO4 encodes a polyamine transporter, and thus its downregulation in the later stages corresponds to the downregulation of pathways involved in transport and process (Fig. 9) (Albertsen et al., 2003). 

As mentioned before, the thin stage likely represents a transition stage between the initial establishment of the biofilm and the fully formed velum. As such, the thin stage replicates share profiles of differentially expressed genes with the early and mature replicates. This stage also features the shift of yeast away from glycolysis to oxidative metabolism as glucose concentration decreases. Enriched processes include cytoplasmic translation, cellular/aerobic respiration and molecule metabolic processes, and pathways in the TCA cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, and carbon/pyruvate metabolism (Fig. 8, 9). As such, YEL069C/HXT13 is upregulated in this stage as low glucose induces its expression; it encodes a putative transporter that allows for more efficient uptake of sugars when environmental glucose is low (Jordan et al., 2016). In a similar vein, YKR09W is upregulated as it encodes a key enzyme in gluconeogenesis, reflecting the shift towards gluconeogenesis as glucose is exhausted (Zelle et al., 2010). Other genes upregulated in this stage reflect the response of yeast to environmental stressors, with higher expression levels of YCR105W/ADH7 and YDL243C/AAD4. These enzymes both encode for alcohol dehydrogenases involved in aldehyde tolerance and the oxidative stress response (Larroy et al., 2002; Delneri, Gardner, & Oliver, 1999). 

Shifting to the mature stage, upregulated genes correspond to biofilm establishment and maturation, and adaptive responses to depleted resources and stress. At the mature stage, the main carbon source of glucose is all but consumed, forcing yeast to utilize alternative sources. Thus, enriched processes include mitochondrion-related biological processes, processes related to energy derivation and generation (Fig. 8), linking to pathways like the TCA cycle and oxidative phosphorylation (Fig. 9). For instance, YBR117C/TKL2 expression is induced in carbon-limited conditions, as its product is required for carbon and energy scavenging (Boer et al., 2003). This represents the diauxic shift, or the transition from glycolysis to ethanol as the main carbon source (Galdieri et al., 2010). Genes such as YPR127W have been known to be induced by stress, thus explaining its upregulation in the mature stage (Zuzuarregui, 2006). The last broad category of upregulated genes refer to (LIST PATHWAYS), which all contribute to the maintenance of the biofilm. For instance, upregulated genes like YDR085C/AFR1, YOR247W/SRl1, YKL164C/PIR1 all encode proteins for cell wall stability and contribute to the morphogenesis and shape of the velum (Konopka, 1993; Hagen et al., 2004, Mrša & Tanner, 1999). Most notably, YIR019C/FLO11 is one of the most differentially expressed genes in the dataset, being largely upregulated in the mature stage. FLO11's role in biofilm formation has been previously documented (Váchová et al., 2011; Alexandre, 2013), as it encodes a glycoprotein that contributes to cell-cell adhesion and flotation (by increasing cell surface hydrophobicity). Expression of this gene during velum formation is key for flor growth and maturation, as its deletion prevented biofilm formation in yeast (Váchová et al., 2011).

It is key to note that abundance of transcripts does not imply any significant biological effects, as protein abundance may not relate to transcript abundance. As such, further studies must be done relating the transcriptome to the proteome, thus validating the results of the current study. In any case, analysis of the yeast transcriptome through different stages of biofilm formation reveals key genes relating to the biological process and metabolic pathways employed by yeast as it grows and develops.
